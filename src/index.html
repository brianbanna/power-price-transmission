A dispatch from the European grid

The Price of Wind and Sun.

Switzerland produces almost no solar power. Yet on a spring Sunday in 2024, its electricity price dropped lower than Germany's. This is how one country's renewable revolution is rewiring the economics of its neighbours.

Brian Banna · Lê Thào Huyèn · Hajj Hannah

01 A Sunday in spring

At midnight, the grid breathes in rhythm.

Across central Europe, electricity costs roughly the same at the stroke of midnight. Around €45/MWh. Five countries, one price. The grid is calm.

02 Then the sun rises

Germany has forty-two gigawatts of solar capacity.

On a clear spring Sunday, there is more electricity than anyone needs. Germany's solar alone approaches the country's entire domestic demand. Prices begin their descent.

03 Negative

Switzerland dropped lower than Germany.

Switzerland produces almost no solar power. Yet on this Sunday, Swiss electricity hit −€145.12/MWh — ten euros deeper than Germany's −€135.45. The grid doesn't respect borders. When Germany's solar floods the interconnectors, Swiss prices crash too.
